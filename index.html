Global Energy Transition: Fossil Fuels to Renewables

This project will examine how the global energy system is shifting from fossil fuels toward renewables using the Our World in Data Global Energy dataset. Our focus will cover four research questions:

  1. How has the United States' energy use evolved over time?
  2. Which countries are electricity import-dependent, and how have they changed since 2000?
  3. How do major renewable/alternative electricity generation technologies (solar, wind, nuclear, hydro, biofuels) differ in their S-curve adoption?
  4. How does a country’s GDP relate to its use of renewable energy?

Each tab addresses these questions with an interactive visualization.

Data & Methods

Core Dataset

Our dataset is owid-energy-data.csv from Our World in Data. Each row represents a country or region in a given year and includes demographic, economic, and energy variables.

  • Locations: 300+ countries/regions, from individual countries and regions such as “World” and “OECD”
  • Time: Most countries have data from the 1960s–1970s through 2023, with some gaps, particularly for developing nations
  • Attributes:
    • Demographics: Population, GDP
    • Primary energy: Total energy, Fossil fuels, Renewables
    • Electricity: Generation, Renewable share, Carbon intensity
    • Trade: Net electricity Imports & Exports as share of demand

Dataset for Global Map

For the global renewable map, we constructed energy_mix_1980_2023.csv, which stores each country’s primary energy mix by year:

  • Years: 1980–2023, for countries with provided data
  • Columns: iso_code, country, year, renewables_share_energy, fossil_share_energy, coal_share_energy, oil_share_energy, gas_share_energy
  • Logic: If fossil_share_energy was missing, but renewables_share_energy was available, we set fossil_share_energy = 100 − renewables_share_energy and dropped rows with missing key fields

Patterns from the Data

  • Steady rising Global demand. Total primary energy use grows linearly over the last four decades, with small dips connected to global recessions and COVID-19. All transitions from fossil fuels will need to happen while demand is still increasing.
  • Fossil Fuels dominate, renewables 2x global share. The global renewable share rises from roughly 10% in 1980 to about 20% in 2023, meaning that even after four decades, around four-fifths of primary energy remains fossil-based. At this rate, the world will largely fall short of its projected climate goals outlined by the UN and Paris Climate Agreement.
  • Patterns emerge across regions. Nordic and a few Latin American countries now get 50% or more of their energy from renewables, while historically fossil fuel-exporting economies have largely been stagnant.
  • Electricity is concentrated. A majority of countries trade electricity, usually fossil fuels, but only ~61% of countries are within ±5% of self-sufficiency in imports.

US Energy Mix Over Time

This line chart compares renewable and fossil fuel energy consumption in the United States from the 1960s to 2023, in terawatt-hours (TWh)

Interaction: hover over the lines to see the exact fuel consumption for the given year. The red line is fossil fuels; green line is renewables.

The plot shows that US fossil fuel consumption has stayed high - above 14,000 TWh for most of the period with slight declines from the late-2000s. Renewables start from a relatively small share, but begin accelerating after 2005. By 2023, renewables exceed 3,000 TWh, roughly 3-4x its 1980 level, with fossil fuels still representing the majority of energy use. The US represents a common pattern among fossil-field exporting nations - rapid growth in renewables layered on top of an existing fossil system, with slow renewable energy growth relative to fossil fuels.

Electricity Independence - Net Imports as % of Demand

The choropleth map shows each country’s net electricity imports as a % of domestic demand from 2000 to 2023. Purple color represents net exporters, white self-sufficiency, orange net importers.

Interaction: use the slider or play/pause button to move across years. Hover to see net imports, total electricity generation, and renewable share for the year.

A pattern within the global map is larger economies are more self-sufficient for electricity. The United States only imports 0.4% of its electricity demand even though it is the 4th-largest importer by volume, while China exports 0.1% of its electricity production. These country's size allows them to balance their grids and reduce exposure to international energy shocks.

In contrast, smaller countries are dependent on imports from others. Luxembourg imports 79% of its demand, Djibouti 72%, and Afghanistan 87%. These countries may have high renewable shares, but their energy relies on neighbouring nation's grids. Of 173 countries in the data, 105 (61%) fall within ±5% of self-sufficiency, and the median country has 0 net imports, meaning half of all countries are net exporters of energy.

The map also highlights some specific changes with smaller countries. Paraguay remains a large exporter, but its net exports fell from ~773% of domestic demand to a ~121% through the decades. Bhutan moves in the opposite direction, shifting from exporting 337% of its production to importing 7%. Not represented in the chart is how infrastructure and demand fluctuations change country’s energy position, particularly if they are smaller.

Renewable Adoption S-Curves

The multi-chart figure compares global electricity generation from solar, wind, nuclear, hydro, and biofuels. Each subplot overlays the historical time series with a fitted S-curve projected until 2050, to show where each technology appears along the adoption path.

Interaction: hover over the solid and dashed lines to see historical generation values and the fitted S-curve for each energy type. Click on the legend to add/remove an energy type from the chart.

The biggest pattern from the S-curves is solar’s growth over the last two decades. Solar generation was at 0 in 2000 but reached 2,131 TWh by 2023. Wind, which started in the late 1970s, now generates 2,497 TWh, only slightly more than solar. Solar achieved the same growth as wind in half the time, making it the fastest growth renewable-energy represented by the graphs.

Other technologies follow slower or plateauing trajectories on the S-curve. Hydroelectric grew gradually from 923 TWh in 1965 to 4,419 TWh in 2023. Nuclear grew from the 1970s through the 1990s at 2,700 TWh, but has since stabilized. These disparities, particularly nuclear, represent how each energy type's S-curves are affected by economics, policy, and public opinion, which were not represented in the dataset. Biofuels grew modestly in comparison with all other energy types, connecting to their geographic-influenced rate of usage.

The graphs suggest wind and solar are in the growth phase of their respective S-curves, with no visible flattening with their technological maturity. Nuclear in particular is unclear as its growth was based on policy, infrastructure, and social acceptance, of the respective era, with global adoption fluctuating after the end of the cold war.

GDP vs Renewable Energy Share

The bubble chart plots each country’s GDP per capita against their renewable share of primary energy for 2022. Bubble size is total primary energy consumption, color is region.

Interaction: hover over a bubble to see the country name, GDP per capita, renewable share, and energy consumption. Use the legend to toggle regions on/off.

The chart shows no noticeable relationship between wealth and renewables as a share of total energy. Countries with similar GDP per capita have renewable shares ranging from 0% to 70%, representing a break from the notion that GDP, GDP per capita, or GDP growth determine a country's use of renewable energy.

Region-to-region patterns are present. In Northern Europe, high-income countries such as Norway, Iceland, and Sweden have GDP per capita above $50–60k with renewable shares in the 50–70% range, while other European nations with GDP per capita between $30–60k cluster by 10–40% renewable share, despite being in the same region. South America shows several lower-middle-income outliers, with GDP per capita at a lower $15k, but renewable shares near 50%. This represents a break from the assumed high GDP to high renewables norm.

In Asia, larger energy consumers stand out as a result of their large populations. China and India have lower renewable use at 0–20% each, at larger total consumptions, representing a heavier reliance on fossil fuels. This suggests there could be a relationship between population and scope of fossil fuel usage. The Middle East presents as a cluster of high GDP per capita countries with a lower renewable energy share. This region in particular uses oil and gas, which can be tied to their global presence in fossil fuel exports through OPEC. North America, especially the US and Canada have higher energy use but more modest renewable energy shares, with the US at 11%. Africa has lower overall energy usage, with renewable energy usage different across nation's borders. Oceania provides another contrast, with Australia combining high GDP and low renewable share, and smaller, Pacific nations having lower GDP and higher renewable shares comparatively.

From the data, we can identify that there is no direct correlation between a country's GPD & GDP per capita and renewables as a share of total energy usage. Instead, smaller relationships could be found on a region to region basis, implying features not represented in the dataset, such as culture, policy, and geographic differences may be a stronger indicator of renewable energy implementation.

Global Renewable Energy Map

This map uses energy_mix_1980_2023.csv to show each country’s renewable share of primary energy between 1980 and 2023. Dark purple represents low renewable shares, bright orange indicates high renewable shares.

Interaction: drag the year slider to move through time. Hover over a country to see its renewable share, fossil share, and split between coal, oil, and gas.

The map summarizes over 40 years of the global energy transition. In the early 1980s, most of the world is dark purple, with 80% of primary energy coming from fossil fuels, and having only 0–20% renewables. Over time, the colors in Western and Northern Europe and parts of Latin America slowly brighten. By the mid-2000s, Nordic countries such as Sweden and Norway, along with hydropowered nations in South America, show renewable shares above 50%.

The transition is much slower in other areas. Large parts of Asia, the Middle East, and North America remain dark into the 2010s, reflecting a continuing fossil-fuel dominance and reliance. Some African countries such as Kenya and Ethiopia brighten, while others remain largely unchanged. The United States improves modestly but is still darker than most of Europe in 2023.

Globally, the renewable share rises from 10% in 1980 to 20% in 2023, meaning that the world is still far from a renewable-dominated energy system. Nordic countries show some of the largest improvements, with changes from 10–20% renewables in 1980 to over 50% today, while fossil fuel-exporting economies in the Middle East show the least change. Most progress occurs from 2000 to 2020, as the map brightens across Europe, parts of China, Latin America, and a few Asian and African countries.

Conclusions & Future Work

Key Findings

  • Renewables are growing, but still a small share of energy generation. Solar and wind have reached the 2,000–2,500 TWh production range in just a few decades, but fossil fuels still supply roughly 80% of global primary energy.
  • Electricity independence is the norm, but not for smaller countries. 61% of countries are within ±5% of self-sufficiency, but a subset of small economies import 70–80% of their energy needs, making them energy-dependent and unable to respond to possible global events and supply shocks.
  • Energy-Technology lifecycles differ. Hydroelectric and nuclear energies show matured, plateauing S-curves, while wind and solar are still in the rapidly growing.
  • Wealth does not determine renewables usage. High-GDP countries are widely represented across all renewable shares (0–70%), and no strong relationship can be found between a nation's wealth and their use of renewable energy.
  • The global transition is geographically spread out. Northern Europe and areas of Latin America stand out as leaders in renewable energy use, while North American, Asian, African, and Middle Eastern nations lag behind.

Look-back & Next Steps

  • Our analysis uses country-level data to draw relationships, but sectoral, regional, cultural, and sub-national breakdowns would better reveal where decarbonization is actually occurring, allowing us to identify the features that cause renewable energy growth to be stronger in some areas.
  • The S-curve fits are simple, and noisy, particularly with matured technologies. Integrating uncertainty bands may help give a stronger prediction of future growth, as well as including relevant policy/event markers for each.
  • We treat “renewables” as a single category in some areas. Identifying firm versus variable renewables (i.e. Solar vs Nuclear), and creating more in-depth breakdowns for each country would help us find the specific growth by renewable type.
  • Future work could combine the visualizations with more complex statistical models and alternative data for unseen drivers such as policy, fuel prices, and total investment flows to move from simple correlation toward a more complete explanation of the green energy transition globally.
